The Last Gunner: No Way Out

PCVR

The Last Gunner: No Way Out is a wave-based survival shooter built in Unity 6, developed with a five-person team for PC and Meta Quest. Set during an alternate WWII overrun by a gas-mutated infection, the player is welded to the turret of a tank disabled by an anti-tank mine, defending it alongside Anatolij, a young mechanic sent out on supply runs between waves. The player is stationary and can only rotate, a deliberate constraint that also keeps camera movement VR-comfort safe, while resources scavenged by the mechanic feed a crafting system that keeps both of them alive as the horde scales up indefinitely.

Role in the project

Across the five-person team I acted as the project's VR and game designer, owning the hands-on VR implementation, the core game and enemy design, and the audio system, on top of modeling and texturing the main assets. Day to day, that meant:

  • VR weapon interaction (dual-grab reload handle, magazine physics, recoil)
  • Game & enemy design (core loop, wave scaling, four enemy archetypes)
  • Narrative writing (staged dialogue system between the two characters)
  • 3D modeling & texturing of principal assets (tank, weapon, enemies)
  • Audio system architecture (FMOD buses/VCAs, spatial SFX)
  • Art direction (low-poly + pixel-art visual identity)
  • Enemy AI setup (NavMesh-driven horde behavior)

Core loop & turret combat

The player is fixed inside the tank's turret, sharing one ITurretController interface implemented separately for PC and VR so both platforms drive the same combat logic. The mounted machine gun is modeled after the MG34 with a double-drum magazine, a historically grounded way to justify the 75-round capacity without needing a belt-fed reload in VR. Scoring reads hit location off each enemy's hitbox, rewarding headshots and distance over point-blank body shots.

NPC companion & AI

Anatolij, the mechanic, is sent out to scavenge and returns automatically or on command, handing off whatever he picked up to the player's inventory. While outside he can be grabbed by the horde, triggering a timed rescue event the player has to resolve by shooting the attacker without hitting him. Back inside the tank he mans a front-facing automated machine gun, and the two characters trade scripted dialogue across five story beats that I wrote to carry the narrative without cutscenes.

Crafting & survival economy

Every scavenged material feeds a crafting system built around scriptable-object recipes, each combining two dropped items into a buff for the mechanic (bandages, plated armor, stimulants) or the turret (ammo, damage rounds, faster reloads). Crafting is only available while the mechanic is inside the tank, since he's the one actually assembling everything, and a run's score can be converted into currency for a meta-progression shop between matches.

Enemies & infinite waves

Four enemy archetypes, each designed with a distinct combat read: the slow Farmer whose pitchfork punishes the mechanic, the Crawler that climbs onto the tank to steal loot before fleeing, the Bloated zombie whose pustules detonate into an area attack, and the Spore zombie that heals nearby enemies. A horde controller escalates enemy count and drop rates wave over wave with no hard difficulty cap, and every run's score, kill count and round reached are saved to a local leaderboard.

VR interaction & audio

  • Dual-grab drum magazine with a physically driven reload handle and matching recoil feedback
  • Custom extensions on top of XR Interaction Toolkit for offset grabs and drop-height adjustment
  • FMOD-driven audio architecture with independent buses/VCAs for master, ambience and zombie SFX
  • Dozens of simultaneous spatialized enemy and environment sounds mixed through the same bus graph
